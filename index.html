Alfred Hitchcock

Film Director, Producer, Screenwriter, Pioneer

Alfred Hitchcock is considered by many to be one of the greatest movie directors of all time. He is a pioneer in many aspects of filmaking and earned the nickname "The Master of Suspese". Not only was Hitchcock a master at creating supsense and terror, he was also a technical innovator. Many of his camera techniques are used by the most prominent directors today still.

The Life and Times of Hitchcock

  • 1899: Alfred Joseph Hitchcock, the second son and the youngest of three of William Hitchcock and Emma Hitchcock, is born in London.
  • 1914: Alfred's father passes away. This would have a huge impact on his life being that his father was his only friend. His relationship with his mother was strained.
  • 1915: Starts work as a technician with Henley Telegraph and Cable Company before transferring to the advertising department. He is exempted from war service because of his weight and his employer's involvement in the war effort.
  • 1920: Hitchcock gets a job at a studio designing title screens for silent films.
  • 1925: Michael Balcon of Gainsborough Pictures gives Hitchcock a chance to direct his first film The Pleasure Garden. The movie is not as succesful as projected but it gets Hitchcock's foot in the door as a promising director. The release of The Pleasure Garden marks the start of a nine-film collaboration between Hitchcock and screenwriter Elliot Stannard.
  • 1926: Hitchcock's second film The Lodger: A Story of the London Fog is released to critical and commerical success and Hitcock's star begins to rise in the movie industry. He also marries his longtime wife and personal assistant Alma Reville who he stays with until death. She wrote many of his his screenplays and was involved in most of his movies.
  • 1928: Hitchcock and Alma have their first and only child Patricia Hitchcock.
  • 1929: Hitchcock's movie Blackmail, Britain's first sound-on-film production premieres at the Regal Cinema in Marble Arch.
  • 1934: The Man Who Knew Too Much was released, and was one of the most successful and critically acclaimed movies of Hitchcock's British period.
  • 1935: Considered one of his best films of his early times, 39 Steps was released, starring Robert Donat and Madeline Carroll.
  • 1939: Hitchcock and his family leave London and head to Hollywood where he signs a deal with producer David O. Selznick for a seven year contract.
  • 1940: Hitchcock's first American movie, Rebecca, is released and becomes another hit commerically and critically. Buys the 200-acre Cornwall Ranch near Scotts Valley in the Santa Cruz mountains of northern California.
  • 1942: The first of two films made for Universal Studios, Saboteur, starring Priscilla Lane, Robert Cummings, and Norman Lloyd, releases. Hitchcock did extensive location shooting, especially atop the Statue of Liberty, a first in Hollywood. Hitchcock set the precedent of using famous landmarks in films. He repeatedly did this in his movies.
  • 1943: Hitchcock's mother dies after a long illness. His brother also dies. Shadow of a Doubt is released. The film would later be selected for preservation in the United States National Film Registry by the Library of Congress. This was Hitchcock's first movie to be added to the National Film Registry. Hitchcock stated that Shadow of a Doubtwas his personal favorite of his movies.
  • 1945: Transatlantic Pictures, a production company was founded by Hitchcock and business associate Sidney Bernstein. However, this company dissolved after two commercial flops, Rope (Hitchcock's first Technicolor film) and Under Capricorn. Hitchcock's business venture was unsuccessful, but he produced all his films after this.
  • 1946: Notorious is released and has remained one of Hitchcock's most acclaimed films. The movie plot about Nazis, South America, and uranium cause some trouble for Hitchcock. Because he used uranium as a plot device, Hitchcock was under brief FBI surveillance.
  • 1950:Begins working as an independent filmmaker, signing various contracts with different studios.
  • 1951: Hitchcock releases Strangers on a Train, his first collaboration with cinematographer Robert Burks who will go on to shoot all but one of Hitchcock's remaining films.
  • 1954: The critically acclaimed Rear Window is released and would go on to become one of Hitchcock's most thrilling and most exciting pictures. In 1997, the movie is selected for preservation in the United States National Film Registry by the Library of Congress.
  • 1955: 'Alfred Hitchcock Presents' TV series debuts on CBS.
  • 1958: Vertigo is released. The film stars James Stewart and Kim Novak, who both give superb performances in one of Hitchcock's most psychologically complex thrillers.
  • 1959: North by Northwest, starring Cary Grant, Eva Marie Saint and James Mason, is released.
  • 1960: Hitchock releses the legendary horror classic Psycho through Paramount.
  • 1965: Hitchcock and French director Francois Truffaut agree to an ambitious book project in which they will discuss and analyse Hitchcock's entire film output from the 20s to the present day
  • 1979: The American Film Institute presents Hitchcock with its Life Achievement Award at a ceremony at the Beverly Hilton Hotel attended by, amongst others, Cary Grant, Jane Wyman, James Stewart, Ingrid Bergman, producer Sidney Bernstein and studio head Lew Wasserman, and French director Fran√ßois Truffaut.
  • 1980: At age 81, Alfred Hitchcock dies of arthritis, liver failure, and slowdown of kidney function. On this day, the world lost the father of thriller films and the innovator of cinema. Never again will another director take control of the movie industry like Alfred Hitchcock.
